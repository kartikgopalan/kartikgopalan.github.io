Just Passing By America

(August 28 – September 8, 1999)


Kartik Gopalan

Here's the story of my 12 day journey from New York to San Francisco in September 1999. As I started writing, I realized the account is going to be so huge that none of the hapless readers is going to have the patience to finish it all in one sitting. So I've written it in 12 parts to hopefully mitigate any indigestions!

Starting Off


So you can say I almost did it! My second dream was to drive across U.S. to Grand Canyon. Aim for the stars and at the least you'll land on the rooftop. Well I drove across U.S. all right, but by the time I reached near Grand Canyon, I had seen so many other canyons, mountains, and rocks on the way that I wanted to see no more of them for some more time. So I went straight to Las Vegas instead for a grand finale! Even though the mission didn't get accomplished fully, the trip was one in a lifetime - 12 days, a zillion gallons of gas, staying in every type of motel and camps, tasting every kind of food on the way, and best of all getting to see almost every form of landscapes and people there are to see in this vast country.


So this is how it started. I had to reach Berkeley (near San Francisco) by the middle of September on a project for next one year. Being a grad student in U.S., one needs the penny-pinching mentality to survive here and I was trying to relocate between two of the costliest places in this country. With my bank balance hardly enough to ship my car 3500 miles across the country it didn't take much time for me to figure out what the best option would be to reach my destination - drive myself to San Francisco. (just for records - my advisor did reimburse the travel costs, as best as he could later but of course I didn't know he would :)


Well, wanting to do something interesting is one thing. Getting a consensus on doing it is another. Why do you need a consensus for a single person? Well you do especially if you are the youngest in the family with many guardian angels looking after your welfare. Honestly, I am glad and thankful I have so many guardian angels and going by their advice, I needed to find an experienced partner to travel along. I needed to leave by September 1st to reach San Francisco on time and with just a week left for before my trip, there still wasn't anyone willing to come along. I couldn't expect much either. With the new semester just getting kicked off, who would like to miss classes, drive 3500 miles across the country, and fly back immediately to achieve a net displacement of zero in terms of distance but a big displacement in terms of bank balance?


They say that if you want something really badly, miracles happen. And a week before the D-day I found just the person who would do all of the above except miss classes - since he had just graduated from being a grad student to teaching these poor souls. Pedro was a Ph.D. student in our lab and graduated just a few months earlier. Like all Professors, he had decided that academic life was more interesting than real world! So he went back to his teaching position in Portugal. Now on vacation in U.S. he had plans to go further north to explore the wilderness of Canada. But desperate as I was, I gave it a long shot and asked him to drive with me instead across the country. Ask and thou shall receive - I have never seen anyone agree more promptly :) Rest is history.


Coping with my crazy last week at summer internship, I managed to get my car tuned up and wrap my belongings at Stony Brook. Me and Pedro paid a visit to local AAA office asked the poor guy at the office to carefully plan out two separate routes across the country for our drive from NY to SF in 10 days visiting as many interesting places as we could. Shaking his head in despair, the AAA man took out a big map of united states, marked out every road we'd have to take on the way (most of which we never followed) and carefully placed green circles along the path on places that would be interesting to stop by.


My car is a '95 Hyundai Accent 2-door hatchback and it was a feat of extremely careful engineering to have both mine and Pedro's belongings packed into the pseudo-trunk and the backseat of the car. Every item had a specific place to fit in and nowhere else to go. A loaded 2-door hatchback is not the best companion to go long-distance travelling, but it was the best we had and it proved to be good enough. The route we chose was close to the longest one could take to reach a place diagonally across the country. First we were to go west half the way from NY, then north-west towards Yellowstone further away from our destination, then straight down south to Grand Canyon and then up north-west again to San Francisco. Armed with tourbooks and campbooks for almost every state on the way, and a big National Geographic Road Atlas, we set out on our mission.


27th August



The day before we left, me and Pedro visited the local malls and bought an odd assortment of items ranging from a hammer, some spanners, cooling bag, a rain repellent coating for car's windshield, and lots of eatables for the way. In my hurry I had forgotten to buy a camera, but since I had a disposable one for the moment from my earlier visit to a Mets/Cardinals game in NY, I decided not to worry about it till later in the trip. How could one miss out a camera from shopping list before leaving on such a trip?? ... well, that's me :)

Mad New York – Sleepy Pennsylvania – Gates to Chicago


28th August










Saturday morning of 28th Sept. we were all set to go. I bade goodbye to my housemates and we drove off to the nearest gas station where I bought a gauge to measure tyre pressure. It was a nice, sunny and perfect day to set out on the trip. We took the Northern State Parkway out of Long Island into New York City. Once we joined the road leading to George Washington Bridge, the going was really slow at the rate of 10 miles per hour. It was to be the slowest drives in the whole trip. I am not a great fan of New York traffic and was glad to be getting away from it for a long long time. Driving on the upper deck of GW Bridge, I got my last sight of the impressive Manhattan skyline.


We took Route 80, which actually goes all the way west to San Francisco if you bother never to get out of it. But that's not really what an adventure is made of, is it? Our destination for the day was to reach my brother's place in Penn State University by the evening. The drive through New Jersey was absolutely boring. Only thing good about the drive is that the gas prices are one of the lowest I have seen anywhere in U.S. But then they offset it by charging tolls once too often. Once we decided to stop for lunch, it was impossible to find an exit with a decent food joint. Finally we located a Diner and Pedro decided to try out American chilli for the first time. It proved to be hot in both ways - spicy and burnt his tongue! I stuck to a good old omelette. Another stop at a CITGO gas station, which was run by a sardarji, and we entered Pennsylvania. This part of the trip was much better with a great drive through the lush green hills. I started clicking away with my disposable camera. It was more or less an uneventful drive the first day and we reached State College by 7:00 in the evening where my brother Venkat ( or Venky as we call him) and his wife Veena stay. Veena had cooked a great multi-course Indian dinner in anticipation of our arrival. Her cooking skills are absolutely incomparable and this was the last proper dinner we were to have for rest of the trip! The VVs showed great interest in our trip plan and the places we were to visit on the way, and Venky's refrain was that he was envious of and happy for me as I was doing what he had long wanted to do.


29th August.





Early morning next day, we bid our goodbyes, bought some foodstuffs and drove out of State College with the arrangement that I was to call VVs every night wherever I stopped on the way. See...guardian angels :) It was a long drive out to the border of Pennsylvania as the state is really wide! Route 80 itself is quite beautiful in this stretch but we decided to explore further and took side roads, which basically ran parallel to the interstate but passed through countryside towns and villages. There were signs of deer crossing all along the roads, but the only deer we saw were those that had been run over by some passing vehicle. Too bad. Towards the western border of Pennsylvania, the hills began to fade away and were replaced by a more regular terrain.


Next we entered Ohio and we were again on the interstate 80, with traffic much sparser and consisting mostly of trucks. Pedro and myself had been swapping our driving turns whenever we stopped for gas, food or at rest areas. The rest areas for public are really neat along the highways, something that impressed me a lot. Some even have free coffee for sleepy drivers! Neither of us had really stretched the vehicle very much, partly because of the speed limit and also because we were aware that it was loaded - you could say it was "back heavy". On the stretch of Ohio, the limit got higher around 70 mph and I figured out that a loaded Hyundai could do 80 mph plus with ease. With good reason Pedro kept warning me to slow down. Pedro: "After reaching San Francisco, drive at whatever speed you want, but not on the way!”


Me: "Yes sir!”


We had been on the interstate for too long and wanted to take some side roads. Pedro decided to navigate by intuition and gave me directions to drive in the general direction towards north.


Me: "Hey, do you know where exactly we are on the map right now?”
Pedro: "No." But don't worry. Just keep driving as I tell you?”
Me: "Have you been here before?”
Pedro: "No."
Me: "Huh?!"


And so we went on, driving through a lot of side roads. For the benefit of those readers outside U.S., each road in this country is identified by a number, as you must have figured out by now. I choose not to list them in this account for obvious reasons of that being uninteresting...so you'll find the phrase "we drove through lot of side roads" recurring throughout my accounts.


Suddenly I found myself driving by the shore of what appeared to be a sea? For a moment I was zapped since I thought we were driving towards the middle of U.S. Soon I figured out that this was Lake Erie, one of the five in Grate Lakes region and also the source of famous Niagara Falls that I had visited a few weeks earlier. I now gained a healthy respect for Pedro's sense of direction. Later throughout the trip, whenever we entered a new township or city, I'd give over the controls to Pedro. He would locate the downtown for the town in no time using his `sixth sense' and take us where the action in the town really was. Cool.






The shores of Lake Erie seemed to be inhabited by rich people. There were many private beaches and sporting activity seemed to abound. Quite a beautiful area. We reached Sheldon where there is a protected area called "Brown Marshlands". I had never seen a marshland before so this was a new experience. This area is known for rare birds, insects and plants. The air was filled with sounds of crickets. Walk along a mile of narrow road took us along the sides of the marshland. Brown surface of marshes were absolutely plain without any frills and from a distance, it was hard to tell whether it was solid land or just some dirt covering the surface of a pond. The road led to Lake Erie. We hung around for a while around an abandoned NASA pumping station by the shore and while running to escape some waves, I almost disposed off my disposable camera into water.


Found our way back to route 80 and just drove then onwards towards Elkhart, Indiana where we had booked a room in Best Western Inn for the night. Another feature peculiar to the stretch of roads this far was that we had to pay tolls very frequently all along route 80. The more toll they charged, the worse the roads used to be. Thus far, all trucks in Penn and Ohio used to stay on the right lane and travelled well below speed limit. This gave me the impression that truckers in this country are really well behaved and care about safe driving. Once we entered Indiana, it seemed as if cops don't patrol these highways at all. For the first time in the whole trip, we had trucks overtaking us at 90 mph. More trucks overtook us at higher speeds in this stretch than anywhere in the whole trip did. And for those who don't know, trucks in this country are really big and long.... not like the rickety small ones that speed on India's highways. Passing by a truck, you feel like you are passing by a train ... okay I am exaggerating but that kind of sums up the way it feels to be driving besides one of these dinosaurs.




Anyway, we reached safely at Elkhart. The inn was nice and had even had an indoor swimming pool. Pedro, a swimming enthusiast, had his face lit up at the news but soon changed his mind when he saw that it was a small pool and even that had a bunch of kids jumping around in water. We moved into our room, checked out the weather for next day which seemed okay, but had rain predicted later in the week in our journey, had dinner outside and planned our trip next day to Chicago and Madison.

Chicago’s Cows & Sleepy Madison


So here's Part III of the trip - my visits to Chicago and Madison. I intended to cover more but this itself proved to be pretty long. Looks like its going to take more than five parts:) Enjoy the reading! - Kartik


Monday 30th August

We started off from Elkhart after a continental breakfast at the inn and headed straight to Chicago. Chicago and New York had very similar histories. Both are supposed to have started as an industrial township. Chicago basically had coal and oil industries. It was also the centre of labour struggles. Later, as happens to all industrial towns, Chicago and NY evolved to be strong financial trading centres in U.S. As we got into the outskirts of Chicago, it had dull grey and uninviting looks with industries dotting the horizon. There were huge power cables going into the city from all directions in the sky and innumerable trucks heading into the city from all directions on the road.


We drove further inside the city where people seemed to have houses and soon were on neat roads. We drove on one by the side of Lake Michigan and stopped for a while by the lake to get a view of the Chicago skyline. In lakeside areas people seemed relaxed and laid back even at 11:00 in the morning of a Monday. No one seemed to be in a hurry. The main feature of the city was the distinct skyscrapers. Each was amazingly shaped. The typical Chicago architecture seemedChicago 2.jpg toChicago 3.jpg have tall buildings with sharp tapering tops piercing into the sky - each one making a loud statement of its glory.


We parked our car in a public parking and started walking around. I took lots of pictures - mainly buildings. Since I was still using my disposable camera, one of our goals in Chicago was to buy a good camera. Pedro seemed very excited and rushed around from street to street as if he knew the place. I got a good exercise chasing him! In the process I saw a lot more of downtown Chicago than I would have seen by myself. A funny and queer thing about Chicago is that they have statues of cows all over the downtown. Each cow is painted with funny colours and patterns. One had all its internals illustrated like assembly line of a factory where milk is produced and the process it goes through! Some sense of humour!


After a lot of running around, we visited a local AAA office to get more maps, and I finally bought a proper camera - a simple point and shoot Konica with zoom and some extra focussing features. We left Chicago around lunchtime with lot of crowd getting out of their offices and headed towards Madison in Wisconsin. Wisconsin didn't seem to have many big cities. The only other prominent city seemed to be Milwaukee. Madison itself is a very small city. It has the state capitol whose dome dominates the city skyline according to city's laws. The stretch of Capitol is sandwiched between two big lakes and is very beautiful. Small residential area surrounds the capitol building. The whole city seemed extremely laidback and sleepy. The University of Wisconsin at Madison too is by one of the lakes. Again we parked our car and started walking around the streets and visited the banks of each of the lakes.





As the evening hours started approaching, the streets became much livelier. A lot of undergraduate crowd, probably freshmen joining in the Fall, appeared on the streets from nowhere and soon it was a party atmosphere by the shopping streets and restaurants. A lot of houses belonged to fraternities and sororities named as Alpha Kappa Lamda, and whatever combinations of any three Greek letter you can make out. Fresh kids in college hung out on the balconies and shouted across to each other. One of the houses appeared burnt - probably a drunken Saturday night party!


We left Madison around sunset and headed towards La Crosse where we planned to stay for the night. Part of the way, we took a smaller side road and were rewarded with the sight of a beautiful countryside with glorious sunset over its cornfields. Unfortunately, I had no more film rolls with me: ( We passed through Baraboo and Sauk City which were small sleepy townships with an economy depending on tourists and passing traffic on the highway. Went by a casino which had amazingly large number of cars parked in its parking lot for the sparsely populated countryside. For the first time we had a taste of night driving for a couple of hours. We reached La Crosse at night. It’s a small town by the border of Wisconsin and Minnesota. We checked into a motel, had dinner at Pizza Hut and went off to sleep. Plan for next day - drive across Minnesota - "The Land of 10000 Lakes".

Cowboy Towns: Something for you to remember us by!


Here's the account my drive across Minnesota. A day in which I didn't see any big places, but had a lot of fun anyway.

- Kartik





Tuesday 31st Aug

Woke up at La Crosse and left the motel after breakfast. We picked up some more eatables form a local grocery store where the people were friendlier than I had seen so far in the trip. We roamed around a sleepy township, which wasn't up even at 10:00 a.m. We went to the banks of junction where three rivers -Mississippi, Black, and La Crosse - meet each other. There was a huge statue of Hawaiatha native Indian by the river. So I stood in front of the statue and Pedro took a snap of an alien Indian standing in front of a native Indian! Also went through a small museum, which had the history of La Crosse. Apparently the French were the first from Europe to arrive in the area and they set up lots of lumber businesses around the place. Mississippi River served as a means of transporting the timber to downstream locations.


We drove out of La Crosse and into Minnesota. The number plates on the car ahead of us declared "Minnesota - a land of 10000 lakes". Indeed we saw many lakes along the places we passed by. Northern part of the state is supposed to be more scenic and from the maps it appeared to have more lakes. But we were driving along the southern border of Minnesota and this region was mostly great plains and prairies. It was miles and miles of farmhand as far as eyes could see. Around noon, we stopped by a small mid-western town named Albert Lea. It was a somewhat run-down small township besides a lake. This town had many flat roofed concrete buildings, many of them painted bright red, which was different since most towns in eastern side have wooden houses with tiled, tapering roof. We went to a local post office where Pedro wanted to mail something. While he was in line, I had a good time observing a confident and cute 2-year old native Indian kid giving his mom a hard time by trying to pull off all booklets and envelops within his reach.


We checked out a small family-run pizza shop. The hostess was nice and friendly. Apparently not many foreigners or "out of towners" come to this town. So she asked as to where we were from. When I mentioned Long Island, she immediately started talking about the JFK Junior's plane crash near LI and how sad she felt for him. JFK Junior seemed to be a heartthrob among women in America. More than once I have heard American women reminiscing about him fondly. The pizza was good and we tipped her well!


Off we drove out of Albert Lea towards a place called Sioux Falls. We didn't know whether there were any falls at all. Tour Book mentioned a Falls park in the city. So we went. About 50 miles before Sioux Falls, we decided to take a small sideroad. This road had no signs as to where it went or where all those turns lead to. So we stopped by a small run down gas station. The area seemed quite poor with mostly local trucks stopping by. The girl at the station
filled the gas and cleaned the car's windshield. When I asked for directions to Sioux Falls, she called her friend from inside. The reason I mention these details is that this second girl had green eyes - the greenest I have ever seen in a human being. That combined with her serious looks appeared really spooky. While she explained the route to us in a heavy mid-west accent, the first girl got a souvenir for us from inside - it was a plastic tablet with the name of the gas station painted on it. "Its something for you remember us by. We don't see that many out of towners around here!" Pedro and me had a small contest later as to who gets to keep the souvenir...I did!


Pedro wasn't convinced that the directions we got took on through an interesting route. So as soon as we got out of the gas station, he promptly took another route and drove by intuition. As always, his intuition was right. We passed by beautiful farmlands and reached Sioux Falls City. This was a somewhat bigger city with much more activity around evening time. I was still wondering if there were any falls around the place. We took directions to the falls park and came across a small waterfall with a nicely maintained park around it. There wasn't much water around this time of year. We hung around for a while and climbed a tower nearby to get a view of the city. Sioux Falls started off as an industrial township that grew around the Big Sioux River. Now it was a prominent business centre. There used to be a seven storeyed wheat processing mill besides the water fall in early 20th century that derived its power using water from the falls. They still had the ruins of the mill. Pretty cool stuff for that time.


Drove around a little bit more and left the city by taking a small side road. Somewhat rough road with no traffic in sight for miles and miles. Just plain farmlands. At places I saw some storage towers and farmers' houses nearby. I was wondering how it would be living in one of these places and had a strong urge to stop by one of these farmhouses and stay there for a week or so in their lifestyle. But that would have to wait for sometime later. Right
now I was in a rush to go across the country and could only hope for a glimpse of this remote life. I was rewarded with another beautiful sunset with a purple sky and this time I promptly took photographs.


We joined interstate 90 and drove to Chamberlain. It’s a town by Missouri River - basically a tourist attraction. We had not booked a place this night so we hunted around till we came across a somewhat expensive inn called Cedar Shores just on the scenic banks of Missouri. Pedro and me were in a dilemma whether to take the room at such an expensive place and decided it was probably worth it. Resolved to camp the following nights to make up for it! I took a shower at the room, had dinner, and I went out to the river shore while Pedro decided to go off to sleep. After spending some time by the shore watching the reflection of town's lights in Missouri's flowing waters, I went back to the room resolving to get up early the next morning to catch the sunrise.

South Dakota: Fossils, Presidents & Gambling


Wed. 1st Sept.


Woke up to a great morning at Chamberlain in South Dakota along the banks of Missouri. I got up early, got ready, and had coffee along the riverbanks. On the other side of the river Chamberlain town was enveloped in mist that hung halfway through a bridge across the river. Went for a walk alone to shoot some photos of Missouri waking up lazily to sunrise. I wasn't disappointed with what I got to capture in my films. I went back to the hotel room for a quite breakfast while Pedro scoured the beautiful riverbanks for some more time.





Soon we were driving westwards again on interstate 90 towards Badlands. This stretch of South Dakota is again basically lots of farms and grasslands. As we kept driving along the monotonous terrain, I began wondering whether the famed beauty of South Dakota was overhype. But one thing I learnt on this trip is never to underestimate the power of this land to shake you out of slumber with the power of magnificent contrasts and surprises. I came upon the contrast I wouldn't forget in a hurry. Amid miles of grasslands and farms, a group of naked cliffs jut into sky to present an amazing contrast. These are the Badlands, very strange looking bunch of mountains having veins along the sides created by ages of erosion. The native Indians and later the French independently named it as Badlands since they couldn't use the land for any purpose. Later this place was found to be rich in dinosaur and other
ancient fossils and became a protected region. This terrain very much resembles the Chambal Valley in India, probably only more colourful. Our first sight was a splendid overview of the Badlands from the periphery. Soil erosion has caused the volcanic sediments to erode over half a million years and exposed various layers of soil along the sides of the hills – red, yellow, maroon, brown, violet (!). Great place for photography. A thin stretch of well maintained roads took us on a loop around and into Badlands with many places to stop for scenic overlooks. There were many hiking trails going into the heart of the hills and I wished I could stay longer and go along one of these - but I was just passing by this place – couldn’t afford to stay longer.


We left badlands towards Rapid City. Badlands followed us a long way and slowly faded into grasslands. We drove into Rapid City around 3:00 p.m. and Pedro quickly located its downtown with his action sensing radar. This place seemed to have less population than Sioux Falls City but had more activity and liveliness. This place had lots of shops and streets with German sounding names but I couldn’t figure out if it had any German historical influence.
Lots of students seemed to be walking around in streets, many with painting canvasses and musical instruments. We entered a small restaurant and ordered Cafe Mocha and sat observing the strange place. The restaurant was a bright place with had lots of modernist paintings hanging on the walls. A shelf in the corner had a bunch of books on philosophy and art. On one side were a bunch of computers with somebody trying to set up network connection among them. A student sat in a table in front of us reading seriously. Someone upstairs was practising loud guitar, again and again going over the same tune trying to get it right. There was a bunch of CDs on sale with songs from some local band. A group of teens entered the place who seemed very friendly and happy - they seemed to know everyone around the restaurant ... this seemed like a local hangout for these kids more than a restaurant. A happy place that felt good and which somehow stood out in my memory of the whole trip.


We left Rapid City and entered Black Hills nearby where there's Mount Rushmore with the faces of four presidents carved out on a steep rock cliff. It was close to evening and Black Hills looked really black - they are thickly wooded with dark green pine trees. We encountered steep curvy mountain roads for the first time in the trip and this was the beginning of a long mountain-driving test that my car was to survive through for the rest of the trip. We reached Mount Rushmore and had a look at the faces of presidents from various vantage points. It seemed like such a crazy idea to carve out faces of presidents on a rock cliff in a virtually no-man's land. Apparently this was a big national project at the time it was done and South Dakota's official slogan now read "Great Places, Great Faces!". There was even a mini demonstration by a guide that explained how the faces were sculpted on the rock face.
It began raining and we decided to head out of the place. First drive through rain in our journey. Black Hills are a great place to drive through. We stopped at scenic Potoma Reservoir that fed water to Rapid City and surroundings.


Our next stop was Deadwood. A small township, but very strange place. Situated in the middle of Black Hills, it is more of a tourist township with a thriving Casino business. It started out as a gold mining township and soon gambling became the favorite pastime for the folks. They have legends all over the town about different great gamblers who inhabited the town and their famous stories. There was a monument for one such guy who, the stone claimed, was shot to death by some other gambler. The nightlife in the town was just picking up when we arrived there. Many casinos had flashy cars outside as rewards for winning a jackpot. Many old men and women dressed fashionably roamed around and seemed to be having a good time at these places.


Driving out of Deadwood and onto the interstate, I felt a strange feeling - like I was Alice in Wonderland - jumping into one rabbit hole after another with a different fairyland in each hole, a different experience. I was entering one strange place after another, each a completely unique emotional experience and quickly getting out of it, all packed into such a short period of time. Felt like I was looking at all this from way up above in the sky, and it seemed such a strange world to have such different things packed in a small place called Earth! Interstates roads seemed like the only a long, lonely and sane places on this planet. We drove into Spear Fish where we had a room booked and rested for the night.

Welcome to Wyoming: “Like No Other Place on Earth”


2nd Sept

Great Day. Got out of Spear Fish. It was a cloudy morning and was threatening to rain. We drove into Wyoming with our final destination for the day being Cody. At the border of Wyoming a board declared "Welcome to Wyoming: Like No Other Place On Earth!". And true to its name, I haven't seen a place like this anywhere else. Our first encounter with Wyoming's nature was a stretch of low misty clouds that we ran into for a long stretch. In the limited visibility, the stretch of land I could see had yellow and wavy appearance. The trees and hills had a ghostlike appearance amid the hanging clouds. I don't have enough words to describe the morning as it was but it had a bewitching beauty. And since Pedro was in charge of the wheels, I got myself busy clicking pictures even through the dew that started forming on the windshield.



We drove through Gillette, which is an oil and coal town. Like all industrial towns, it had a shabby appearance. An unmanned gas station there wouldn't accept my credit card. A railroad station nearby didn't seem to have a soul around the place. We next drove towards Buffalo, which sits at the tip of Rocky Mountain ranges. Another small
Midwest town which had legends of war between native Indians and westerners. Most Indians got butchered in the wars and now the surviving descendants live in reservations. It had a war memorial for everyone from the town who had died in a fight ever since the American civil war. Had lunch at a restaurant called Busy Bee restaurant. It was a small homely restaurant where lots of old timers seemed to hang around. Everyone knew everyone else and we seemed to be the only strangers around the place. It was a particularly cold day that morning and I had to pull out my sweaters. Pedro had apparently come unprepared for this cold - something that struck me later as quite strange for a person initially planning to travel in Canada! But he insisted on roaming around in shorts and T-shirt! Brave man :)

We stopped for coffee at another shop. The girl there was friendly and while preparing our Cappuccino she told us stories about her trip to India and China. "So where are you guys heading to?". "San Francisco". "Wow! That's along way from New York. So, you'll be visiting the whole of United States on the way. How many days are you planning to take? A month?". "No just 10 days. We are just passing by." And that's where I got the title for this
series - "Just Passing By America"!


We got directions to go to Thermopolis from the girl and headed out of Buffalo. Soon we drove into Big Horn national forests. These were similar to Black forests with mountains at high elevation. As we went higher, we ran into thick cloud cover. Visibility was poor again but it seemed like a dream driving through those clouds
with only ghostly outlines of the forest and the mountains visible to us. At the highest elevation (exactly 9666 feet!), we got a great clear view of the hills. From this point onwards, we kept driving downhill. Suddenly we found ourselves driving through the middle of a creek with steep rocky walls jutting out on both sides and a small stream flowing through the middle. This, I later found is, the Powder River Pass which is at the entrance to the town of Tensleep. We just had to stop and get out on many of the pullouts along the road where I took many pictures of
the creek. The road wound down along its walls and finally passed through the bottom of the creek providing views of the creek from every angle.


Immediately after Tensleep, we drove through a long stretch of Route 16 between Tensleep and Worland. This stretch presented a stark contrast to the lush green Bighorn forests. At first sight I thought it was a desert. In fact these are the badlands of Wyoming. The land here is completely yellow and barren with very little vegetation. I did not see such a desolate stretch of land again till I reached Nevada desert. Many oil wells dotted the horizon here pumping oil from as deep as 10000 feet in the ground.


After Worland, we reached Thermopolis - my first sight of hotsprings. The Continental Divide is basically a fault in earth's crust on American continent that runs from north to south all across. As a result, streams originating on the eastern side drain in the direction of the Atlantic Ocean whereas streams originating on the western side drain towards the Pacific. We stopped by one of these locations where there were actually two streams, one draining in east and other in west. Absolutely cool effect! Again I got myself `Patel` photographed against the signpost, pretending to hold the divide together.

Next we drove further to the tip of canyon - a place called "The Temple of Sinawava". It’s a semi-circular area surrounded by high rocky walls with small streams flowing in from around to join the Virgin River. The place was absolutely packed with cars with no parking space and people having picnic all around the stream. We couldn't get a parking spot there and decided to head back through the canyon. Halfway through, we stopped to take a 2-mile trail to a set of three pools called the "Emrald Pools" - the upper, middle and lower pools. I proceeded ahead alone as Pedro went searching for something at a nearby lodge and said he would follow me. The trek took me along the rim of the canyon and provided great overlooks of the rocky faces of the canyon from various points. Pedro caught up with me pretty soon. We visited the middle and the lower pools. One had to trek 3 miles further to reach the upper pool and there was no time for that. I would rather call them "puddles" of water since there is little water flowing along the streams at this time of the year.


But the trek peaceful. Back at the bottom of the canyon, we had coffee at a lodge while looking from the windows at a magnificent view of a towering red rock face of the canyon in front. It was getting close to evening now and our aim was to reach Las Vegas by night if we were to get to see its famed nightlife. We wanted to book a room in advance of getting there. There were at least a thousand rooms in each of the big lodges or hotels listed in the tourbook. We finally located a hotel that suited our budget. Apparently it also doubled up as a casino, but it was supposed to be one of the lesser mortals in the city of the rich and mighty. So from the bottom of a deep canyon in the middle of a nowhere forest in Utah, I dialled a number 150 miles away in a desert city called Las Vegas and booked our rendezvous for the night!


We drove out of the Zion Canyon and took the nearest interstate freeway # 15 heading towards LV. After crossing the Utah border, we drove through a brief 30-mile stretch of Arizona. The landscape now consisted of absolutely barren grey hillsides. For a stretch, we had to drive westwards and the glare from the setting sun would blind us from the front. I had a hard time keeping my car in the lane. The speed limit here was 75 miles per hour and people easily drove at 90 or 100. For our loaded Hyundai driving on a hilly road, even 65 is a great feat! The traffic was pretty heavy. There were signs, which warned that these highways were patrolled by aircraft. Apparently the cops just take note down the registration number of speedsters from up in the sky and mail a ticket to your home address..nice, you get roasted and you don't even realise...at least till later :)





Once we entered Nevada, the landscape became more valley-like with barren hills on the horizon. We drove through miles of desert having just small bushes and shrubs for vegetation. At one point, we realized we were low on gas and needed to refuel. I was warned that in Nevada, you could drive for a couple of hundred miles without encountering any sign of habitation, let alone gas stations. Alarmed, we took the next exit, which showed gas with an arrow pointing into nowhere in the forsaken desert. Many miles into the exit and we still couldn't trace even the faintest sign of any town or village. After about five miles, we reached a small town by the side of a small pond in the middle of the desert. Gas prices were, of course, sky-high. While refuelling, got to peacefully watch a beautiful sunset over the desert skyline. Small reward for long detour! Drove back five miles back on the highway towards LV.


By the time we reached the outskirts of Las Vegas, it was night. In order to get into the Vegas mood, we decided to tune into the local FM stations. Immediately I was hearing the sounds of Frank Sinatra filtering into car. Sinatra is apparently a legend in this city, which breathes his music every day. Ironically, one of his numbers, which I heard repeatedly here, was "New York! New York!" :) As the highway entered the city from over the hills, down beneath in the valley, in the center of a desolate desert, there were a million glittering golden lights of the city. It was an amazing and crazy sight. It required an extremely high level ingenuity and insanity for someone to land up in the middle of nowhere in a desert and decide to erect the biggest and most famous gambling city in the world with its two hundred thousand inhabitants making a living out of this business.


As crazy and fantastic as it appeared from a distance, it appeared more insane as we drove into it. My first impression of the city was that of a clown with a perpetually smiling facemask to entertain his daily train of customers. There was glitter and pomp everywhere. Every neon sign was for some casino, hotel, motel, free buffets, million dollar gambling prizes and what not. Every inch of the exterior of entire buildings were illuminated with bright colored lights to attract maximum attention. Even grocery stores and gas stations had slot machines lines up so that people could try their luck anytime, anywhere. We checked into our hotel (which had a decent number of slot machines lined up from the entrance to the dining cafe).


It was a pretty good room for a cheap price. The food and lodging in this city are supposed to be cheap so that people can spend more money gambling. Freshened up with a hot shower and had dinner at a nearby Indian restaurant called "Gandhi Restaurant" - poor Gandhiji turning in his grave. Finally we walked onto the streets to have a taste of Las Vegas. Many `Noveau riche` of the city drove around on the four-lane city roads in flashy convertibles, classy limousines and loud motorbikes. Every third car had loud music on its stereo, mad look in the its driver's eyes, and speed in its wheels. A merry and happily drunk couple was swaying on the sidewalk. The crowd knew how to dress for the night. Men young and old, in formal suits, gelled hairs, cigars in mouth and stern straight look in eyes. Women, young and old, in flashy dresses, high heels, fat purses. And, just like in any other big city of U.S., there was other part of the crowd too - homeless people in rags, mostly blacks and Hispanics, hanging around street corners, accosting passers-by for small change, handing out advertisement pamphlets or just lost in the world. Towering over this melting pot of people were the tall flashy glittering multi-storeyed casinos. Ballagio was a casino with a sprawling garden.


There was a musical fountain show every half-hour in which fountains danced to the tunes of Frank Sinatra's songs. Caesar's Palace was another one in which they try to recreate the original palace of Caesar. Different areas in this five-star casino were designed to give the effect of walking through the open streets of Rome and were called by Roman names. The amount of money they put in to maintain this world of make believe is amazing. Paris was a casino built in the shape of Eiffel Tower. The interiors resembled the streets of Paris. The ceiling was so painted and lighted to give the impression that we were walking under open skies. The employees and even the customers wore typical French dresses. Young women looking like French models sat along the benches of this make believe streets with cigars in their mouth and exuding typical cool looks of the French. Another casino was called New York. It had two buildings, one resembling the Empire State Building and the other resembling the Chrysler building. There were bridges with conveyer belts connecting different casinos to each other. This way people could move around between casinos easily and the owners of casinos happily shared the money these customers had to offer. The main playing area looked the same at all casinos. Young and rich men with their women sat at table games and played high stakes Old retired people sat alone at slot machines, spending away their life's savings quarter by quarter. The continuous music emanating from slot machines filled the background and sounded like incantations for money. The night shows in Las Vegas are another famous feature. We checked the prices but it was more expensive than we thought and we decided against it. What's visiting Las Vegas without a little gambling. So I played exactly a dollar at one of the slot machines. Each of the four quarters dutifully vanished into the machine like a black hole! It had been a long day and we stumbled back to our hotel room for a good night's rest.


Goal for the next day - cross the Mojave desert and hit Santa Barbara on the West Coast!

Tue 7th Sept

Left Las Vegas in the morning and drove into Mojave Desert. Our aim was to cross the width of California and hit the West Coast. This was going to be the toughest test for my car thus far. We packed a lot of water, filled the gas tank to the brim and headed west out into the desert's wilderness. The desert stretch was more tiring than any other. It was a hot day, but we decided to use the air-conditioner sparingly in order to save on gas. The highway went in a straight line for miles and miles through the middle of the desert. At times we saw mirages of water far away. The land was more of hard barren brown stretch of flat ground with sparse shrubby vegetation than sand. It was afternoon when we hit the first town, Barstow, after a few of hours of driving. Filled car's gas tank and stocked more water again as a precautionary measure and as we headed out of Barstow, we drove on the historic Route 66 for a short stretch. Now don't ask me what's historic about it ... the only reason I knew about this route is because there is a brand of Jeans that carries the name "Route 66"! I guess this must have been one of the early routes to the West Coast. On the way, we passed through a customs checkpost. This is where Californian authorities check passing vehicles for agricultural materials. Apparently they are extremely paranoid about genetic materials from outside entering the state. We were just waved off to pass on. Had we been checked, it would have revealed a small pack of apples and pears. I honestly could not understand how they could enforce such a regulation in a foolproof manner.





Next we set out to cross the remaining stretch of Mojave. We passed by the Edwards Air Force Base, which sprawls over a rectangular stretch of desert. We passed by many air force and army vehicles on the road. I pulled out my binoculars to peep at the various military structures just in case I got to see something interesting. Finally I could locate a line of what looked like transport aircraft. We approached Mojave town, which had a stretch of barren hills in the background. The hillsides were dotted with hundreds of windmills. I have never seen so many windmills in one place. Presumably they supplied power to the air force base and Mojave Township down below in the valley. At Mojave town we turned south on a road that led directly to Los Angeles City. Sixty miles north of LA, we headed west again into Antelope Valley. Slowly life and vegetation began to show signs again. A canal with crystal clear blue water was being used to cultivate the hillsides.





Finally we hit the Coast Range of Californian Mountains. It was my first taste of Californian life when we stopped at a Mac Donald’s in Gorman for a late lunch. People appeared more relaxed and friendlier than where we were coming from. It was definitely sunnier than where we were coming from. Southern accent - definitely more twisted than where we were coming from. Its "all rightie!", not a straight "all right". (I later found that even my friend from India who had arrived here not very long ago had caught on to this expression!). As my English professor at Stony Brook had pointed out, it promised to be a different country altogether. All rightie ... here we come CA! We decided to drive in through Los Padres National Forest. It was miles of scenic and also some of the most difficult mountain driving thus far. We stopped at a point called Valley Vista , which provided view of a brown hill below with ridges spread into neighboring valley like fingers of a gigantic human palm. Mountainsides were covered with yellow grasslands with cows grazing peacefully. Amazingly there were lots of private properties within the national forest. We lost our way very soon on these unmarked roads and kept guessing our exact position in the map. When it approached sunset, we were still entering and exiting Los Padres repeatedly and I began wondering if I would get the sight of the Pacific Ocean by the night.


Fortunately we came back on the tracks and at dusk we reached Ojai (pronounced Ohai... you get the idea..here "j"'s don’t have an identity of their own :). We stopped for a coffee. More "all righties" at the shop counter. It seemed to be a small rich town. People here seemed to be fitness freaks. Young boys and girls were out for their evening jog while the older lots were having brisk walks. We decide to park at Santa Barbara for the night - the site of the all too famous TV soap show by the same name from which I used to shrink back in disgust while my sister watched it religiously everyday! But I was quite eager to see what this town was all about after all. Pedro, who had been there before, promised it was something to see. So we headed on route 101 along the coastline and I got the first sight of the Pacific Ocean in my life. As we approached Santa Barbara, the sun had already set in the horizon and had left a reddish tinge across the skyline. All places marked on the highway had Spanish names and Pedro appeared to be quite happy in the familiar surroundings. Whenever I came across a Spanish name he would gladly translate its meaning to me. Thus I learnt that Santa Barbara and San Francisco were actually names of early missionary saints in this region.


The first thing I noticed in Santa Barbara were the palm trees along the roads. There were hardly any multi-storeyed buildings. Most buildings were built with Spanish architecture - which got duly identified courtesy Pedro's expertise - and were painted with creamish color. After a bit of hunting around the town, we located an international youth hostel called "Banana Bungalow". It was a kind of yuppie place run by a German guy who said he had travelled all over the world before deciding that Santa Barbara was the place to live in. The accommodation was cheap at $20 a person per night. It was a small two storeyed regular house which had fancy painting all over it and had been torn up and redesigned inside to give an appearance of chaos which a typical college student loves to live in. The place was more of a dorm with bunkered beds - eight in our small room. There was internet access in what was once a living room and had now been fitted with more beds. There were shared cooking facilities where a group of students was busy cutting vegetables. Pedro and me dumped our luggage, had dinner at a Subway, and headed into the town.


Our first stop was the pier along the ocean - a wooden platform that jutted into the sea. It was a nice hangout place with restaurants and bars. Pedro was especially happy since the whole town had Spanish culture imprinted everywhere. Most streets have Spanish names. Pedro was quick to point out the few buildings that did not conform strictly to Spanish architecture. The State Street was the hub of nightlife. There were pubs dotting every block where youngsters hung out having a good time dancing and drinking. Someone sat on the sidewalk playing a pathetic tune on his guitar. A number of homeless people hung around in corners even here.


We woke up early morning on Wednesday and had a walk through the town in daylight. Santa Barbara waking up to the morning was much different than at night. It was somewhat of a foggy morning at sea. After seeing the garden in their courtyard we headed back towards the sea. The shore had many bikers, skateboarders and early morning joggers. After spending some time on the beach, we headed out of Santa Barbara in our last leg of the journey towards our final destination - San Francisco!

Welcome to the “You Can Never Leave” California

Wed 8th Sept. '99

Out from Santa Barbara, we drove north on route 101 along the shoreline. We passed through a lot of farms - stretches of flat green cultivated land which resembled the rice fields of South India. Most of the farm workers were Hispanics and had a rustic look , which was quite different from any people I had seen in other parts of this country. After passing by Santa Maria, we stopped at Pismo Beach along the rocky shoreline and had our lunch while watching a group of wave surfers. This was the first time I was seeing anyone wave surfing. It’s a curious ritual to watch. The surfer would patiently lie flat on his surfboard for something like 10 to 15 minutes turned towards the sea and waiting for the ideal wave. When the surf came, he would turn right around, ride the crest of the wave and when it just began to break, he would deftly stand up on his board and slide along the breaking sides of the wave till he tumbled down with the crashing water. Hmm...so many things to learn.


We left the surfers to their sport and moved further north through San Louis Obispo to join US route 1 of California. This narrow route runs right along the Californian coastline with mountain peaks on one side and the hillsides dropping steeply into the sea on the other side. Along the ridges, where the hills met sea, there were many small beaches. Some were private, while others looked like they had never been stepped upon. Fog rose from sea level, travelled along the hillsides, up the mountaintop and merged into the clouds. The road twisted and turned along the rim of the mountains all the way. Since the road was so narrow, one could either drive or look, but not both at the same time. One mistake and you drive down the slopes to one of the beaches down below, where you basically have to sunbathe till a chopper comes along to pick you up and put you back on the road up above! Just kidding...driving wasn't that bad but was reasonably challenging. We stopped by at many turnouts and took pictures.


There were private properties at weird locations along the hillsides. One of the houses was constructed on a wooden plank jutting out of the slopes onto the sea. The driveway from the highway to the house was a steep drop along the incline. Nearing Monterey, we could see a thunderstorm few miles away in the sea travelling parallel to us. Numerous lightning flashes went all the way to the surface of water. I hoped that the storm wouldn't cross our way till Sunnyvale, which, by the way, was where we were planning to camp for the night at the house of my friend V. Narasimha Rao (VNR as everyone calls him). On the way, we got a view of Hearst Castle perched on the top of a hill. It looked like one of the old castles of England; Pedro told me that some millionaire built it by the same name who didn't know what better thing to do with the money he had. Apparently it’s supposed to be a spooky place and there are trips from the bottom of the hills that take visitors through the castle. We didn't have much time today to share with the ghosts that inhabited this place and after promising them we'll come back later to scare their wits out, we moved on.


Drove further up north into Santa Cruz. This was a small town, which looked like a slightly run down middle class town, not as well off as Santa Barbara, and mostly inhabited by Hispanics. We tried to locate the downtown. Signs were really confusing and pointed towards downtown in all directions. Finally we found ourselves by the seashore which had a pier, a beach with folks playing volleyball, some restaurants and a fake casino where people didn't play with real money. Quite a peaceful place on a cloudy evening except for a little while when all seagulls started screaming their throats out for reasons only they knew. Finding out way out through the maze of signs in Santa Cruz, we headed towards Sunnyvale to VNR's house. A bunch of towns around San Jose - Sunnyvale, Palo Alto, Cupertino, Mountain View, Redwood, Milpitas and Fremont - along with Stanford University comprise the famed Silicon Valley. As we entered the Silicon Valley, there was a tangible change in surroundings. The ads on the radio started chirping about anything-and-everything-dot-com with each one offering some or the other form of internet service. Offices along the way belonged to some software company or the other. Silicon was in the air. All signs indicated this placed belonged to the hi-tech minded. Sunnyvale seemed to teeming with Indian and Chinese software professionals.


I could see more Indians and Chinese around than Americans. But for the Spanish and English names of the streets, one can easily mistake this for another country. This was a spacious locality. Even the side streets were three lanes wide! Something unimaginable around New York area. Following the directions I had got from VNR, we located VNR's house. VNR was my ex-colleague at Wipro Infotech in Bangalore and like every other Indian software professional, he is here making his customary pilgrimage to the Silicon Valley working with Veritas. But unlike most others, he steadfastly refuses to apply for a green card and wants to go back to India. We were meeting after two years and after introducing Pedro, and reminiscing about old times and catching up on who is where, we headed out for dinner. Another friend of VNR, again ...you guessed it...a software professional from India who was new to U.S., joined us and we went to ...you guessed it...an Indian restaurant :) Pedro was used to Indian cuisine by now - courtesy Stony Brook's Curry Club - and promptly looked for Saag in the menu. After dinner, we went home and I dozed off like a log. Next morning VNR left early for office. We packed up and left for Richmond which is where my future apartment was located.



On the way we saw the San Francisco skyline and passed by exits, which pointed to Berkeley. After moving into my apartment, me and Pedro headed for a trip to San Francisco. Crossed the bay using the long San Raphael Bridge and headed south on 101 south towards the famous Golden Gate Bridge.
