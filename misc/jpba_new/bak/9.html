Sun Sept 5th
------------

In the morning at KOA kampgrounds by Bear Lake, I peeped
out of the tent just to have the cold chill hit my face.
That really woke me up. There was a thin sheet of ice on top
of our car. Pedro said it was too cold to sleep, whereas I
had slept like a log. Someone in a tent opposite to ours
was still snoring heavily. Another couple nearby were cooking
their breakfast and endlessly washing their vessels
by a tap nearby(they were still doing that when we left the
site half an hour later). Rest of the camp was up and alive.
All sites were packed with RVs and tents, kids running around
in tri-cycles, pets being let free in a small fenced yard
for their morning ablutions, and humans getting ready for
their share of water sports at Bear Lake. We got ready and
headed away from Bear Lake towards Salt Lake City. The road
away from town climbed up a hill and at the top we got a
magnificent view of the clear blue Bear Lake.

This was a long drive through Utah. This part of the country
has cities, towns and villages situated in one valley after
another. Roads mostly passed through valleys and crossed
a brief stretch of hills before entering another valley.
Most towns are really small, often with a population of a
few hundred people. One of them had a population of 17
(seventeen)! These towns seemed to have been built around the
highways and each would typically last for a mile's stretch
along the road. Each town had a sign board declaring
"Historic Site" with no other description and pointing in
some general direction into the town. We started seeing
these signs with such regularity that it became a game
for me and Pedro to locate towns that did not have a
"Historic Site"!

One of the towns had tall monastery with two rising
pillars overlooking a scenic valley. We figured this must
be a Mormon temple. About 70% of the population of Utah
belongs to a special sect of Christianity called Mormons.
In early 1800's,  their religious leader - Joseph Smith -
got revelations when he was 14, organised a band of followers
in Ohio and moved to Jackson, Missouri which he called
Zion - some similarity to the Promised Land of Israel.
They moved further to Illinois as they got persecuted
by governments.  Later a section of the group split and
were led by Brigham Young on a difficult trip to the
Great Salt Lake  in Utah where he founded the Salt
Lake City.

So with much hope, we drove into Salt Lake City. The lake
itself lies to the northwest outskirts of the city. We decided
to spend time in the city instead. The whole city appeared
deserted on the labor day weekend. All shops
were closed, only a few homeless people on the
streets - dead city. We parked our car and decided to walk
around. Just a week ago, a couple of blocks in the center of the
city had been hit by a tornadoe. I was hoping none would
hit while we were around! The city appeared to be a rich
place by all appearances. Neat clean streets, tall
buildings with fancy exteriors - just no one around. Only
the homeless lying without purpose on the streets belied
its richness. It was like one of the desolate cities they
show in Star Trek in which Captain Kirk and his crew try
to find adventure.

        And though the shadow of a sigh
        May tremble through the story,
        For `happy summer days' gone by,
        And vanish'd summer glory ---
        It shall not touch with breath of bale
        The pleasance of our fairy-tale.
                        (..courtesy Lewis Caroll)

The main and perhaps the only attraction of the city is
a huge Mormon temple. We decided to visit that later.
First it was lunch time and we were hungry. We hunted around
for a restaurant but all were closed. In the process we saw
the Town Hall of the city where only visible souls seemed
to be dozing off under the sun in the lawns. More fancy buildings
and empty streets - actually it felt good walking through the
deserted streets, but I wouldn't want to live here. Finally we
located a Greek restaurant in a street corner where the Cchef
told us that city was like this every weekend. I ordered
a Calzone while Pedro ordered a Pizza. We were the only
customers in the restaurant, yet it took half an hour to get
hold of the goodies.

        Hush-a-by lady, in Alice's lap!
        Till the feast's ready, we've time for a nap:
        When the feast's over, we'll go to the ball--
        Red Queen, and White Queen, and Alice, and all!
                        (..LC again)

It felt like we were caught in a  time warped city where
nothing happens. After an unending lunch, we headed towards
the Mormon temple. The temple has a grand entrance and has
neat interior compound. Visitors are not allowed into the
main temple but there are a few prayer halls around where
one can enter and even take pictures. There were many guides
all too eager to help with questions. One of them was taking
visitors on a guided tour and I joined the group in anticipation.
She took everyone's introduction and then stood  there silently
and smiling benignly waiting for something. I thought Pedro was
getting restless to move, so I left the tour for next time.
Getting out of the temple, there were a group of teenage
hippies with colored hairdos and rings sitting on the
pavements. Suddenly one of the girls yelled at me "You know
what? I hate this city. This city sucks!". Dumbfounded
at this unexpected outburst directed at me-total-stranger, I
decided to it was wiser to leave this claim uncontested
and moved on to a nearby Border's Bookstore where me
and Pedro had coffee. Following that, we drove
out of Salt Lake City as quickly as possible.

Again it was a long long drive through one scenic valley
of Utah after another. We camped in Pedro's tent at KOA
kampground in Beaver. Beaver is located just north of
Zion National Park - our destination for the next day.
